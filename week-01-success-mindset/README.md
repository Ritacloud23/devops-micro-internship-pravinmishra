# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

### Rules

* No "safe" answers.
* Must be your real belief (not copied from internet).
* Minimum 50 words.

**Hint:** What do you believe about career, money, learning, discipline, relationships, health, success, life, tech industry, etc. that most people don't agree with?

## Answer

In learning, I believe that it’s okay to learn slowly, even though most people around me think learning should be fast and immediate. Many people believe that if you’re not progressing quickly, you’re falling behind. There’s a lot of pressure to finish courses, get results, and “figure life out” as soon as possible. But I think learning takes time, and struggling is part of the process. When I move at my pace, I understand things better and feel more confident in what I’ve learned. In life, rushing often leads to stress and comparison, while taking your time helps you grow in a more meaningful and lasting way.
While, in life, I believe that there is no single timeline everyone must follow, even though many people around me believe success has a fixed schedule. A lot of people think you should achieve certain things by a specific age, finish school, get a good job, be financially stable, or have life figured out. I don’t believe that’s true. Life is unpredictable, and everyone’s journey is different. Taking a longer or different path doesn’t mean failure;, it often means growth, self-discovery, and learning what truly matters. Moving at your own pace allows you to make better choices and build a life that actually fits you, not one rushed to meet other people’s expectations.

## Truth #1

### Truth

 Clear systems outperform willpower over time.


### Evidence from my life

 I relied on motivation to stay disciplined and kept falling into the same loops. After creating simple routines and fixed time blocks, I followed through more reliably. The structure carried me on days when motivation failed.

---

## Truth #2

### Truth

Consistent effort compounds faster than sporadic intensity.

### Evidence from my life

 I tried working only when I felt motivated and saw little progress. When I switched to small, daily actions, studying, practicing, or showing up even on low-energy days my skills and confidence improved steadily. The results became visible only after consistency, not bursts of effort.


---

## Truth #3

### Truth

Avoidance increases stress more than action does.


### Evidence from my life

I delayed difficult tasks, thinking it would reduce pressure, but the anxiety grew in the background. When I faced the task directly, even imperfectly, the mental weight reduced almost immediately. Taking action proved less painful than postponing it.

# Reflection – Week 1

### Biggest insight I got about myself this week

I work better with structure and clear deadlines. The DMI Cohort 3 task this week showed me that once there's a defined deadline, I move faster and stay more focused than when a task is open-ended.

### My biggest weakness/loop I noticed

Without a deadline or clear structure, I tend to procrastinate. Open-ended tasks sit longer than they should because there's no external pressure pushing me to start.

### One system I will implement from this week (exact habit + time)

For every open-ended task, I will set my own mini-deadline at the start — writing down a specific day and time to finish it, treating it like a real deadline even though no one else set it.
